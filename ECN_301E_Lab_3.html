Generating Hourly Wages¶

The dataset does not have an hourly wage variable, but has information on usual hours worked per week (jbhrs) and usual monthly pay (paygu).

You will need to generate an hourly wage variable. One way is to calculate hours per month $(=$ jbhrs $\times52)/12)$ and then divide the monthly pay variable by hours per month.

Generate an hourly wage variable

In [9]:
gen hrs_per_month = 52*jbhrs/12
lab var hrs_per_month "Hours Worked Per Month"
gen hourwage = paygu / hrs_per_month
lab var hourwage "Hourly Wage"

Options and Conditional Clauses¶

Getting the hang of when to include a comma in stata commands and when not to takes a while.

No comma is required when you define a logical condition using if
(as above where you defined the logical condition histogram hourwage if hourwage < 30)

A comma is required when you specify further options in relation to a command.
(as above where you specified a normal distribution be drawn on the graph histogram hourwage, normal)

Some more examples of using a comma when specifying options are:
summarize, detail
tabulate region, summarize(totinc)

If you put the logical condition after the option, you will need a second comma to "undo" the effect of the first comma:
histogram hourwage, normal, if hourwage < 30
histogram hourwage, xline(3.6), if hourwage < 30

But, you could, alternatively, put the logical condition before the option and not need the second comma:
histogram hourwage if hourwage < 30, normal
histogram hourwage if hourwage < 30, xline(3.6)

Cleaning the data¶

Some people in the dataset appear to earn less than the minimum wage.

This may be genuine, or it may reflect measurement error in the pay variable or in the hours variable (or in both). In this exercise, you can clean the data in one of two ways.

You can drop the observations with hourly wage $< £3.60$:
drop if hourwage < 3.6

Or you can assume that the problem is measurement error and assign people with hourly wages of less than the minimum wage an hourly wage of $£3.60$:
replace hourwage = 3.6 if hourwage < 3.6

One Sample t-test¶

The one sample t-test is used to test whether a sample mean (of a normally distributed variable) significantly differs from a hypothesized value. For example, you can test whether the average age is equal to $40$.

The command for doing this t-test is:

In [26]:
ttest age==40

One-sample t test
------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Variable |     Obs        Mean    Std. Err.   Std. Dev.   [95% Conf. Interval]
---------+--------------------------------------------------------------------
     age |  12,114    38.86041    .0943042    10.37946    38.67556    39.04526
------------------------------------------------------------------------------
    mean = mean(age)                                              t = -12.0842
Ho: mean = 40                                    degrees of freedom =    12113

    Ha: mean < 40               Ha: mean != 40                 Ha: mean > 40
 Pr(T < t) = 0.0000         Pr(|T| > |t|) = 0.0000          Pr(T > t) = 1.0000

The standard error of the estimated mean is $0.094302$. This is equal to the standard deviation of the age variable divided by the square root of the sample size. You can confirm this by typing:

In [27]:
display 10.37946/(sqrt(12114))

.09430419

The confidence interval provides a range around the estimated mean of plus/minus $1.96$ standard errors. It gives a spread of values for which it is not possible to reject a null at the $5\%$ level. You can confirm that $38.86041 + (1.96\times0.0943042)$, and $38.8601 – (1.96\times0.0943042)$ give the two bounds of the confidence interval.

Confidence intervals are a useful way of presenting means or other estimates, to indicate that there is some sampling error. You can get the confidence interval for any variable mean (eg hourwage) by typing:

In [28]:
ci mean hourwage

    Variable |        Obs        Mean    Std. Err.       [95% Conf. Interval]
-------------+---------------------------------------------------------------
    hourwage |     12,114    9.376863     .052685        9.273593    9.480134

The results of the t-test are reported at the bottom. The t-ratio is $–12.08$, implying a clear rejection of the null at the $5\%$ level. This is consistent with the upper bound of the confidence interval lying below $40$.

The p-value (P) gives the probability that the actual value $(38.86)$ would be observed if the null were true, i.e. if the mean really were $40$. In this case, zero probability.

Also reported are the results from two one-tailed tests: $H_A: mean<40$ and $H_A: mean>40$. In one-tailed tests you restrict the alternatives to be either greater than the null, or to be less than the null. This restriction gives you a lower value for the critical t-statistic $(1.64$ not $1.96)$.

Rather than looking just at the absolute value of the t-ratio, as in a two-tailed test, you also need to look at the sign of the t-ratio in a one-tailed test. If $H_A: mean<40$, you reject the null only if the t-statistic is less than minus $1.64$; if the alternative is $H_A: mean>40$, you reject the null only if the t-statistic is greater than plus $1.64$.

In this case you reject the null if $H_A: mean<40$, but fail to reject the null if the alternative is $H_A: mean>40$. This is because the alternative is even more unlikely than the null.

Individual coefficients¶

The regression output automatically provides t-statistics and p-values for the null hypothesis that the coefficient is equal to zero. Can you reject the null hypothesis that education has no significant relationship with monthly earnings?

Suppose you want to test the hypothesis that the return to an extra year’s education is equal to a particular value, say $\$50$ a month.

In this case, the command is:

In [33]:
test educ==50

 ( 1)  educ = 50

       F(  1,   933) =    3.22
            Prob > F =    0.0732

Stata calculates an F-statistic equal to $3.22$ and fails to reject the null at the $5\%$ level (the critical value with one restriction is $3.84$).

You could have done the hypothesis testing yourself with a t-test, using the estimated standard error: $(60.21 – 50)/(5.69) = 1.79$
(which is the square root of $3.22$)
